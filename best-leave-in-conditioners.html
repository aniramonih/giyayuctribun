The 17 Best Leave-in-Conditioners of 2024

Posted by Tandra Barner on Thursday, August 29, 2024

The shower is made for aha moments, but what happens when your volumizing shampoos and conditioners still aren’t inspiring gorgeous hair? (I.e. you still step out with a dry, dull, and flattened mane.) Leave-in conditioners are a key step in everyone’s hair routine — these products reduce frizz, increase shine, lessen breakage, and help repair split ends and other signs of damage. Contrary to a rinse-out conditioner, one that you leave in for a few minutes before washing away with water, hair stylist and educator Karen Miller explains that a leave-in conditioner "adds moisture and hydrates hair without weighing it down" and should be used "after the hair is cleansed and before styling begins.” 

Now for the catch: Discovering that unicorn pick product that simultaneously moisturizes your specific hair type with nutrient-rich ingredients, but uses a formula that won’t contribute to build-up or oiliness. After consulting several expert hairstylists, we tested dozens of the most popular, buzzy leave-ins out there ourselves to see how they performed across a range of hair types, textures, and concerns. These are our favorite leave-in conditioners for every hair type, concern, and need.

Best Overall

What We Don’t Love: The scent isn’t very prominent.

For a deceptively lightweight, water-based formula, the Crown Affair leave-in conditioner cream nourishes dry strands with a mere half-pump of product and leaves them buoyant, pillow-soft, and frizz-free. In fact, we even skipped applying our other oils and hair serums while using it. Chock-full of plant-based ingredients including tsubaki and meadowfoam seed oils, this natural formula locks in moisture and seals cuticles closed to prevent static and flyaways. Plus, it’s great for adding volume to fine strands according to InStyle commerce writer Irene Richardson. “After a stylist harped on my thinning hair, I self-consciously picked up this gorgeous bottle, and wow, consider it a hair-thickening miracle worker,” she says, adding that her hair air-dried smoother and looked sleeker than before.

Along with the fresh, lightly citrus signature scent of bergamot, yuzu, and lemongrass, which was a little more subtle in this product compared to its others, the smooth semi-opaque matte glass vessel and elegant black typeface add grandeur to the entire experience, too.

What To Keep In Mind

  • Hair Type: While our experts agree every hair type can benefit from a leave-in conditioner, each one needs something different. “Someone with fine hair would benefit more from a mousse rather than something more creamy," says Miller, while celebrity stylist Kylie Fritas recommends a lighter mist for fine and thin hair types. "If your heart is set on a cream leave-in even if you have fine hair, just apply less of it," she says. For hair that is extra dry or damaged, you can bring your hair back to life by applying a leave-in more frequently, or right before going to bed so it can sink in all night.
  • Formula: Out of the three most common formulas — sprays, creams, and serums — the most universal type of leave-in is a spray. “I prefer a spray-in over anything because it allows you to really evenly saturate the hair better than a cream or serum form,” says hairstylist and salon owner Joshua Duchene. However, creams and serums are best suited for thicker, more coarse hair because they need and can handle the extra moisture.
  • Lightweight Ingredients: Since you’re not rinsing out these styling products, Miller suggests looking for lightweight water-based formulas that add extra weight to hair with super-heavy silicones and oils. In addition to moisture-replenishing molecules like hyaluronic acid and glycerin, seek out lightweight oils such as argan, jojoba, almond, avocado, and grape seed. 

Your Questions, Answered

What does a leave-in conditioner do?

We like to think of these multi-tasking hair heroes as a glaze on a donut: They’re the extra element that takes a look from good to really, really great. Leave-in conditioners simultaneously boost hydration, fight frizz, detangle, and offer heat protection, using nourishing ingredients such as plant-based oils and butters. Unlike a rinse-out conditioner, you can incorporate a leave-in conditioner into your post-wash routine by working it through damp hair and styling as usual. 

How do you use a leave-in conditioner?

“I recommend applying leave-in conditioners on damp hair after the hair is properly cleansed — once applied you can move forward with heat styling,” says Miller. Celebrity stylist Joseph Michael adds that you should never reapply a leave-in conditioner to dirty hair because “It can cause a product buildup and leave your hair feeling greasy and thick.”

Colorist Emily Landino recommends using it every other day if you’re someone who washes daily because “it is possible to over-moisturize the hair, which can lead to breakage,” she says.
